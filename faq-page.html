Firearm Laws & Safety Guide

Who Is Prohibited from Owning or Possessing a Firearm?

Per Federal Law (18 U.S.C. § 922(g) & (n)):

Under the Gun Control Act of 1968, it is unlawful for the following individuals to ship, transport, receive, or possess firearms or ammunition:

  • Felons—convicted of any crime punishable by imprisonment exceeding one year
  • Fugitives from justice
  • Unlawful users of—or those addicted to—controlled substances
  • Individuals adjudicated as mentally defective or involuntarily committed
  • Illegal or unlawfully present aliens
  • Those dishonorably discharged from the Armed Forces
  • Persons who have renounced U.S. citizenship
  • Individuals under restraining orders (e.g., domestic violence protective orders)
  • Convicted of misdemeanor domestic violence

Additionally, anyone under indictment for a crime punishable by more than one year cannot lawfully ship, transport, or receive a firearm—but may legally possess firearms obtained before the indictment, unless and until convicted.

How Am I Supposed to Store My Gun?

NRA Guidelines and Recommendations:

  • Store guns so they are not accessible to unauthorized persons.
  • Mechanical safeties are not a substitute for proper storage.
  • Use gun safes, locked cabinets, trigger locks, or cable locks.
  • Store ammunition in a different locked location.
  • Educate children using the Eddie Eagle GunSafe® program.
  • Store in a cool, clean and dry area to maintain the condition of the firearm.
  • Firearms should be cleaned regularly and kept in good working order.

Why are these rules important?

  • Secure storage is crucial to prevent accidental shootings involving children or other unauthorized individuals.
  • Secure storage methods like safes make firearms less accessible to thieves.
  • Responsible gun ownership includes safe storage practices to protect the community.

What Kind of Guns Are There?

  • Handguns: Pistols & revolvers (small, portable, self-defense).
  • Rifles: Long guns with rifled barrels (hunting, long-range shooting).
  • Shotguns: Fire shells or slugs, good for hunting & defense.
  • Submachine & Machine Guns: Heavily regulated under federal law.
  • Specialty Firearms: Derringers, muzzleloaders, short-barrel rifles, etc.

Where Can I and Can’t I Take My Gun?

Federal Law:

  • Schools (Gun-Free School Zones Act)
  • Federal buildings (courthouses, post offices, etc.)
  • Airports (past TSA security checkpoints)

Texas Law:

  • 51% alcohol establishments
  • Polling places on election day
  • Courts & correctional facilities (prisons, jails)
  • School or Sporting events (unless it is part of the event and you are a participant)
  • Amusement parks, hospitals and nursing homes
  • Private property with 30.05, 30.06, 30.07 signs

Can We Share a Gun?

You can share a gun with another legal gun owner in Texas if:

  • They are not prohibited under federal law
  • It stays within Texas (no interstate handoffs without an FLL)
  • It is used for lawful purposes (hunting, range shooting, self-defense)

Never load or share with someone who is prohibited, or you could be charged with "unlawful transfer of a firearm"

Additionally minors under 18 cannot possess a handgun, unless with a parental permission for lawful activities (hunting, target shooting)

Ammo: How Much? What Kind? Where?

There is no single legal requirement, but firearm instructors and saftey organizations recommend:

  • Defensive Handgun (for home or personal protection):
  • At least 50-100 Rounds of defensive ammunition (hollow-point, high-quality)
  • At least 200-500 round of practice (FMJ) for training and proficiency
  • Rifles/Shotguns:
  • Keep defensive loads (buckshot or slugs for shotguns, soft-point or defensive rounds for rifles)
  • Keep extra practive ammo to stay proficient.

What Kind of Ammo? The right type depends on your gun.

  • Handguns:
  • Metal Jacket (FMJ) for Practice/Training
  • Jacketed Hollow Point (JHP) for reliable expansion and stopping power
  • Rifles:
  • FMJ for training
  • Soft Point (SP) or specialized defense rounds for hunting/home defense.
  • Shotguns:
  • Birdshot (#7 1/2 or #8) for practice and clay shooting
  • Buckshot (00 or 000) for defense
  • Slugs for longer range or hunting

Where can you buy ammo?

  • Local gun stores or ranges are best for in-person guidance
  • Big box retailers: Academy Sports, Cabela's, Bass Pro, Walmart, etc...
  • Online at MidwayUSA, LuckyGunner, or Ammunition Depot

Buy locally (stores/ranges) or online (MidwayUSA, LuckyGunner, Ammunition Depot). Check state laws first!

What are the current laws when it comes to pistol braces?

Current Status

  • Federal law does NOT require you to register a pistol with a brace under the NFA (no tax stamp required), as long as it remains legally a pistol (not designed/made to be shouldered like a rifle)
  • Braces are legal to own and attach under current federal law
  • The underlying NFA definition of "rifle" is still undetermined, so if ATF argues a brace is being used as a stock, it could raise issues

What's the difference between a brace and a stock?

Pistol Brace: Designed to strap to the shooter's forearm, helping stabilize a large-format pistol (like an AR-style) without making it a rifle. It was originally intended for disabled shooters.

Rifle Stock: Meant to be shouldered, which classifies the firearm as a rifle. If the barrel is under 16 inches and it has a stock, it legally becomes a short-barreled rifle (SBR) under the National Firearms Act (NFA), requiring registration and a tax stamp.

Why the difference?

The distinction comes down to intended use and classification under federal law. A brace is Legal on pistols because it isn't supposed to convert them into rifles, while a stock clearly does. However, the ATF has shifted its stance over time on braces, and current rules may limit or ban certain brace configurations depending on how they are used.

My husband has a gun now what?

Safe Storage at Home:

  • Always assume the gun is loaded
  • Store firearms locked (gun safe, lockbox, cable lock)
  • Store ammunition separately from the gun
  • If children are in the home Teach them with Eddie Eagle: Stop, Don't Touch, Run Away, Tell an Adult
  • Never leave a gun unattended

Learn the Four Universal Safety Rules:

  • Treat every gun as if it's loaded.
  • Never point it at anything you're not willing to destroy
  • Keep your finder off the trigger until ready to shoot
  • Be sure of your target and what's beyond it

Training & Familiarization:

  • Both you and your husband should know how the firearm works (loading, unloading, clearing jams)
  • Take a beginner firearm safety or concealed carry course in your area
  • Practice regularly at a shooting range. Skill and confidence come from repetition

Legal Responsibilities

  • Know where you can and can't carry. (Federal gun-free zones and Texas specific rules and signs)
  • If your husband carries for self-defense, understand Texas use of force and self-defense laws

Family Communication:

  • Who has access to the gun
  • When will it be carried vs. stored
  • Emergency plans (e.g., home defense situations)

I Have a Gun, Now What?

Learn the Four Universal Safety Rules:

  • Treat every gun as if it's loaded.
  • Never point it at anything you're not willing to destroy
  • Keep your finder off the trigger until ready to shoot
  • Be sure of your target and what's beyond it

Safe Storage in Your Home:

  • Store firearms unloaded and locked (safe, lockbox, or cable lock)
  • Store ammunition separately
  • If children are in the home Teach them with Eddie Eagle: Stop, Don't Touch, Run Away, Tell an Adult

Know Your Gun and Ammo

  • Learn how to load, unload, and clear malfunctions safely
  • Practice with training/practice ammo (FMJ)
  • Keep some defensive ammo (JHP for handguns, buckshot/slugs for shotguns, soft-point or defensive rounds for rifles)

Training and Practice

  • Take a basic firearm safety or concealed carry class (many ranges offer beginner programs)
  • Practice regularly at a shooting range until you are comfortable
  • Dry-fire practice at home (with no ammo present) helps build safe habits.

Legal Awareness

  • Federal Restrictions
  • Texas Restrictions
  • Self Defense Laws

Household Communication

  • Decide who has access, where it will be stored and when it will be carried
  • Make a home defense plan in the case of a threat

I'm Afraid of Guns. What Can Help?

  • Education reduces fear. Signing up and taking safety courses can make it less intimidating
  • Safe storage promotes Peace Of Mind. Keep it locked, unloaded, and separate of ammunition.
  • Exposure therapy at your own pace such as supervised handling of a unloaded gun to learn confidence and control
  • Focus on the purpose, not the fear. The gun is a safety tool, not a threat. It exists to protect you and your loved ones, like seatbelt
  • Emotional support and sharing your concerns with a loved one who may not realize your fear.
  • You don't have to love guns to be safe around them. Go slow.
